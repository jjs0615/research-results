✅ Hypothesis Testing

SUPPORTED

H1: Positive associations

Conservative orientation → higher win orientation (r = .39***), competitiveness (r = .27*), external regulation (r = .41***), and introjected regulation (r = .23*). All significant at p < .05.

SUPPORTED

H2: Negative association

Conservative orientation → lower perceived choice (r = −.33**, p = .002). More conservative individuals reported lower feelings of volition in sport.

Unexpected Finding: Intrinsic regulation showed a significant positive correlation (r = .25*, p = .021), suggesting conservative individuals also experience genuine enjoyment in sport — not predicted by the hypotheses.

💡 Interpreting Non-Significant Results

Five variables did not reach statistical significance: Interest/Enjoyment (r = −.00, p = .982), Perceived Competence (r = .10, p = .350), Integrated Regulation (r = .16, p = .146), Identified Regulation (r = .17, p = .115), and Amotivation (r = .20, p = .074).

The near-zero correlations for Interest/Enjoyment and Perceived Competence suggest that the capacity to experience enjoyment and feel competent in sport may not be shaped by political orientation. Ideological differences appear to influence how motivation is regulated and how competition is approached, rather than fundamental psychological resources for sport engagement.

Note: The study was powered to detect medium effects (r ≥ .30) with 80% statistical power at α = .05. Smaller but potentially meaningful associations cannot be ruled out.
